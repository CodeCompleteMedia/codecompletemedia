Every few months, a client sends us a link to someone promising to triple their
traffic with one weird trick. We understand the pull. The trick is always more
exciting than the truth, and the truth is dull: most "SEO tips" are noise. The
work that actually moves rankings is boring, repeatable, and rarely makes for a
good tweet.

We've watched the clever stuff fail enough times to stop chasing it. The
keyword-stuffed page, the link scheme, the AI-spun article farm: they spike for
a week, then Google catches up and they're gone. What's left standing, every
time, is the unglamorous work nobody wants to do. So that's the work we do.

## Start with the foundation

Before a word of content strategy, the site has to be technically sound: fast
loads, clean HTML, a structure crawlers can actually walk, correct status codes,
sensible canonical tags. Search engines reward pages they can read without a
fight. A page that takes four seconds to load and 404s half its links isn't
getting ranked, no matter how good the copy is.

Most of the wins live down here in the plumbing, and most people walk straight
past it because it doesn't feel like marketing. It feels like chores. It is
chores. It's also where the rankings come from.

## Then write for intent, not keywords

Keywords tell you what people type. Intent tells you what they actually want,
which is usually a different thing. Someone searching "best running shoes"
doesn't want your company history. They want a short, trustworthy answer before
they bounce to the next tab.

So we write the page that answers the real question, completely, and quicker than
whoever's ranking above us. Not the page with the phrase crammed in forty times.
Google has been able to tell the difference for years. Write for the person, and
the keywords mostly take care of themselves.

## Then do it again next month

Here's the part nobody likes: you're never done. SEO isn't a launch, it's
maintenance. Rankings drift. Competitors publish. A page sitting at number two
in March is on page two by autumn if you stop paying attention.

The sites that climb are the ones that keep showing up: fixing the broken link,
refreshing the thin page, catching what's slipping before it falls off. It never
trends and it never feels dramatic. But six months of boring, done every month,
beats one clever campaign everyone's forgotten by Friday.

Boring beats clever, almost every time. We just keep doing the boring thing until
the graph goes up.
