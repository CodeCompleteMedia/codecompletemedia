I use AI every day. I also have one rule about it: **AI _and_, not AI
instead.**

That rule didn't come from a manifesto. It came from watching what happens when
you let the tool drive. Give it enough rope and it will happily build the whole
site for you: fast, clean, grammatically spotless, and completely forgettable.
The work comes out finished. It just doesn't come out good.

## The day the tool almost won

I've felt the pull. Early on, I let AI carry a homepage draft almost end to
end. It read fine. Every section sat where it should, every sentence behaved. I
nearly sent it.

Then I read it out loud, which is usually when you find out. Nobody was home in
it. No point of view, no line you'd remember an hour later, nothing that sounded
like the actual company whose name sat at the top. It was a very confident
impression of a website.

So I rewrote it. Not all of it. The structure was sound, and plenty of what
the tool gave me survived the second pass. But the parts that carry the weight,
the ones a visitor actually feels, had to be made by a person who cared how they
landed.

## Where the tool earns its place

None of that turned me against AI. The fastest path through a blank page is
rarely a blank page, and the tool is very good at getting you off zero. I use
it to draft, to boil a pile of research down to something I can argue with, to
throw up five directions when I'd otherwise marry the first one, and to catch
the small mistakes a tired human misses at the end of a long day. That's real
leverage, and I'm not precious about taking it.

What matters is what I ask it for. I ask for raw material: speed, options, a
rough cut. I never ask it for the verdict.

## Where the human stays

Taste doesn't come from a prompt. The parts you can _feel_ belong to a person
from the first draft to the last pass: the rhythm of a sentence, the restraint
to leave a space empty, the judgment about what to cut. A model can hand you a
hundred options. It can't tell you which one is right for this client, on this
page, for the single thing the page exists to do.

So I keep the final call. Every time. The line of copy, the spacing that finally
feels settled, the choice that makes a page feel considered instead of
generated: those are mine, on purpose.

Used this way, AI makes good work faster. It doesn't make fast work good. Those
are two different things, and keeping them straight is the whole job. The tool
never wins, because I never hand it the one decision that would let it.
